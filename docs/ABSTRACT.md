# Abstract & forum blurb

## Formal abstract (~190 words)

We introduce *inventory transfer*, a reproducible method for comparing the internal structure of an undeciphered text. For each manuscript region, a greedy optimizer learns glyph-pair merges that raise conditional character entropy; replaying one region's merge inventory on another yields a recovery score, interpreted as structural similarity. Applied to the Voynich Manuscript, the method reproduces known glyph groupings, separates Currier A/B at ~77–80% transfer, and maps sections and folios onto a dominant one-dimensional register gradient. The gradient is robust to alternate parses, stochastic and beam search, section holdout, and independent transcribers. Scribe attribution explains little unique variance once section/register is known. Controls show the gradient is real but not semantic evidence: meaningful verbose-cipher text, meaningless section-mixture models, and self-copying generators can each reproduce parts of the geometry. Label morphology contains significant structure, but it aligns mainly with the same register axis. These experiments do not decipher the manuscript; they provide a reproducible constraint that future linguistic, cryptographic, or generative accounts must explain.

## Forum TL;DR (~60 words)

I built a tool that measures the *machinery* behind Voynichese rather than trying to read it: discover the entropy-repairing glyph-merges for one part of the manuscript, then test how well they explain another. Result: the whole text sits on one continuous "register" gradient (84% of variance, one axis), Currier A/B is just a threshold on it, scribes barely explain the folio coordinate once section/register is known (~1% unique variance), the Biological-B extreme looks like a self-copying/repetition effect, and even the labels (the best crib) don't separate like names of what they point at, at least under these tests. Controls show the gradient is real but reproducible by meaningful and meaningless processes alike, so it constrains rather than solves interpretation. Not a decipherment — code + preprint attached, tear it apart.
